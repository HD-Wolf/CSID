Habitus

Like capital, “habitus” is a concept developed by French sociologist Pierre Bourdieu which describes the set of dispositions which appear “natural” or “common sense” to an individual or group.

In computer science education, certain ways of thinking, and certain ways of doing and learning computer science become part of students’ habitus. Doing “computational thinking” (thinking “like a computer”) and learning to self-teach, for example, become part of computer science students’ “natural” dispositions (if they aren’t already thanks to pre-existing capital) [see Rendering Technical].

It is in enacting this computer science habitus that students “become” computer scientists. Students must enact this habitus in relation to both an idealized computer science identity (what Breslin terms the “hegemonic computer science personhood”) and gendered norms associated with it (including norms related to confidence, physical appearance, the enjoyment of gaming and coding for fun, or displaying an entrepreneurial passion for tech).
