Intro

As computer science students (situated in North America), we are taught to value particular definitions of “computer science” and “computer scientist.” Issues of ethics, social justice, and the environment are typically not part of these definitions. Rather, computer science is often presented as immaterial and abstract (mathematical). When the materiality of computer science is addressed (e.g. in software engineering), larger webs of labour and extraction remain hidden. The purpose of this website is to familiarize computer science students with concepts that they can use to think critically about computer science and their own experiences in computer science.


See the Grid or List tabs for an overview of the terms and concepts. Below is a list of questions for students to consider as they start to think critically about their own work and the tech they use and interact with.


Questions to Consider

The following are some questions students might consider as they work:

  • Who is this for?
  • Is this something the users/target audience actually want? Are users involved in the design process? Have you gotten user feedback?
  • Are you coding?
    • Are you writing everything yourself?
    • Are you clearly documenting your code?
    • How much space is it using? How fast does it run? Can you minimize these?
    • Are you using someone else’s code? Do you understand how it works? Have you cited them?
  • What processes of extraction are taking place? (labour? environment? data?)
    • Where is your work taking place?
    • Is it a virtual space? Where are the physical spaces that allow this space to exist?
    • Do you know where the materials come from? Do you know where the materials for those materials come from?
    • Was there waste created?
    • Who is creating these materials (where is the labour coming from)?
    • Is data being collected? How is it being used? Is it necessary to collect this data? What biases might there be in collecting this data? What labour is needed for people to provide this data?
  • What effect might this tech have on other communities?
  • What unintended purposes could this tech be used for?
  • Should it be made?
  • How can I use tech for positive change? Am I addressing the root of the problem, or fixing symptoms?

This list of questions is by no means inclusive, and should be used as a starting place for raising your own questions. Ethics should never be a checklist [see the following for a satyrical paper that makes clear why: A Mulching Proposal by Os Keyes, Jevan Hutson, and Meridith Durbin ].
